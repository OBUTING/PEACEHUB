Kenyan youth · ahead of 10 August 2027

Kenya's peace will be built by the people who show up for it.

Kenya's next General Election is set for Tuesday, 10 August 2027. Every election cycle asks something of us — and youth are almost always the ones asked to carry the tension, on every side. This is a place to build the daily habits that hold a community together, and to say plainly what you won't take part in this time.

Practice, not just principle

Daily practices that build peace

None of these depend on politics. They're habits that make any community — a campus, an estate, a WhatsApp group — steadier, election year or not.

Verify before you share

Before forwarding a message, a screenshot, or a "breaking" post — especially about politics — check where it actually came from.

Talk across the divide

Keep at least one real conversation going with someone who sees politics differently than you do. Disagreement isn't the same as danger.

Name it when you see it

If someone in your group starts using ethnic slurs or dehumanizing language, say something — silence reads as agreement.

Show up on ordinary days

Peace committees, church groups, sports teams, youth baraza — the relationships that hold in a crisis are the ones built long before it.

Protect your peers

If a friend is being pulled toward paid violence or a hostile crowd, pulling them out is one of the most concrete peace acts there is.

Take disagreements to the right table

A grievance handled through chiefs, elders, courts, or the IEBC's dispute process is a grievance that doesn't spill into the street.

What we're up against

What's been breaking peace in Kenya

Kenya has lived through election-related tension before, including in 2007–08 and 2017. None of what follows is about any party or candidate — it's about patterns that have repeated across election cycles, and that youth are often pulled into first.

Tribalism & ethnic profiling

Treating neighbors as threats because of ethnicity, especially once campaign season sharpens identity into a weapon.

Hate speech, online and off

Dehumanizing language — in person, in memes, in group chats — that makes violence feel more acceptable once it starts.

Political misinformation

False or unverified claims that spread faster than corrections, often timed to provoke a reaction before anyone can check.

Paid political violence

Youth recruited and paid to intimidate, disrupt, or attack — often by people who face none of the actual risk themselves.

Vote buying & bribery

Handouts exchanged for votes or loyalty, which quietly erodes the idea that leadership should be earned rather than bought.

Unemployment & economic frustration

Real, valid frustration that gets exploited by people offering quick cash for a crowd, a chant, or worse.
